§1 · Mechanics

How the private market moves an electron.

Every half-hour, the platform attempts to match each kWh of consumption with a kWh of generation from a nominated asset. What's matched flows through the private market at a fixed rate. What isn't gets backstopped — by the grid for shortfalls, by the spill curve for surpluses.

Matched flow: generation and consumption land in the same half-hour. The kWh flows through the private market at the contractual rate — generator receives the PM rate, consumer pays PM rate plus non-commodity charges.

Two flows, not one

Settlement layer · Physical layer

The diagram above shows the contractual flow — the matching ledger. There's also a physical flow, and the two are deliberately decoupled. Electrons leave a generator and travel through whichever section of the local distribution network has the lowest impedance — often metres, rarely the whole country. The private market sits on top, attributing kWh to specific consumers for accounting and settlement purposes. A perfectly matched contract doesn't mean a generator's electrons reached its counterparty's site.

Contractual layer · what UC settles

A direct one-to-one accounting relationship. Generator earns the PM rate, consumer pays it. The kWh appears on both sides of the ledger in the same half-hour.

Physical layer · what the grid does

Electrons leave the generator into the local DNO network. Some travel a few metres to a neighbour, some to the nearest grid substation. The consumer draws from whatever's closest — the matching engine never determines this.

This is why the §4 geographic map shows arcs across hundreds of kilometres — those are contractual arcs, not physical wires. UC settles every half-hour against the metered injection and withdrawal at each MPAN; the laws of physics handle the routing.

§5 · Spill mechanics

When generation exceeds demand.

Generators don't always find a private market match. The 2.19 GWh of unmatched output gets routed through a UC Spill product the generator chooses up front — each tier is a guaranteed percentage of that volume that will be paid at the tier rate. Anything UrbanChain matches to other consumers above that floor is also paid at the tier rate. Anything still unmatched after that flows to System Sell Price.

0%
No volume guarantee. The generator earns the highest tier rate, but only on whatever UC actually matches in-ecosystem. Everything else hits SSP.

UC guarantees two-thirds of spill volume at this rate. Anything matched above 66% is also paid at this rate; the residual goes to SSP.

UC guarantees 85% of spill volume at the tier rate. Higher floor, lower rate.

Full volume guarantee. Every kWh of spill is paid at the tier rate — no SSP exposure, but the rate is the lowest of the four.

How a 100% spill volume is allocated

The tier sets a guaranteed floor. UrbanChain pays the tier rate on at least that share of spill volume, and continues paying the tier rate on anything matched in-ecosystem above it. Only the leftover — the volume UC could neither guarantee nor match — settles at System Sell Price.

Why three rate columns differ: tier rates are negotiated per generator. Each asset has its own connection, balancing position, and forecast quality. Generator 3 sits below the others because its wind profile is harder to balance.

Spill applies only to unmatched output — the matched volume is always settled at the 8.0p PM rate.

§6 · Generator economics

What the generator earns, all-in.

The headline 8.0p PM rate is only part of the story. UrbanChain offers two further revenue layers that sit on top: a REGO purchase at the prevailing market rate (currently ~0.1p/kWh, but tradable and rate-dependent) across all generated volume — a standing offer not taken in this quote — and a revenue share on the P442 license-exempt supply benefit, applied to all volume matched in the UC ecosystem (PM matched plus spill that gets re-matched). The full P442 cost stack is around 6.19p/kWh; for this contract we offered the generator 30% of the components they qualified for — earning them roughly 1.4–1.8p/kWh on eligible volume.

What each layer is

Generator-side revenue components
PM Rate
The contractual rate the generator earns on every kWh matched in real time. 8.0p for all three assets in this deal. The platform fee (0.4p) and any TPI / broker commission are already included in the consumer rate, not deducted from this.
REGO Purchase · UC standing offer
UC buys REGOs at the prevailing market rate — currently around 0.10p/kWh, but tradable and rate-dependent — across all generated volume (matched and spilled). The certificate moves to UC's book; the generator gets a small but reliable uplift. Not taken in this quote.
P442 Revenue Share
P442 is the Ofgem framework that exempts sleeved volumes from a stack of policy levies — FiT, RO, CfD, Capacity Market, and NRAB — worth around 6.19p/kWh at full eligibility. UC captures that exemption on every kWh matched within the ecosystem and shares a slice with the generator. Here: 30%, earning the generator 1.4–1.8p/kWh on eligible volume depending on which components they qualify for.
Eligibility: P442 is available to generators with installed capacity up to 5 MW. Larger generators can still access P442 if they qualify as a license-exempt supply, but the benefit is capped at 5 MW of capacity.
A note on volume

REGO revenue applies to every kWh generated. P442 share applies to all volume matched in the UC ecosystem — both PM matched and spill that UC re-matches against other consumers. Only volume that settles at SSP (genuinely unmatched) is excluded.

§7 · Consumer cost

What the consumer pays, all-in.

The other side of the ledger. Each demand site pays the private market rate on its matched volume, the grid rate on its re-matched volume, and a standing charge that's heavily TNUoS-driven on these HH-metered sites. Across all six sites for year one, that comes to £882k — within 0.47% of a pure grid-only contract, with the standing charge alone accounting for roughly 36% of the total bill before a single kWh is consumed.

§1 · The market shift

Out of wholesale. Into a direct market.

The old energy market was built for big fossil-fuel plants moving energy long distances through the grid. UrbanChain rebuilds the connection — generator straight to consumer, with the wholesale aggregator removed.

The old market
The UC market

The same kWh, sold differently. In both models the electrons still flow over the public grid. What changes is the contract path: instead of a generator → wholesale → power company → consumer chain, the contract sits directly between you and the consumer, with UC providing the matching, settlement and rebate plumbing.

§3 · Pricing tiers

Choose what % to lock.

The single biggest decision a generator makes when joining UC: how much of your output do you want at a fixed contracted price, and how much do you want to ride the UC market? The less you lock, the higher your potential revenue — and the higher your risk. This picker shows the trade-off across the four standard tiers.

The majority of your energy is locked at a fixed contracted price, with the remaining 20% sold at UC market rate (typically very close to System Sell Price). The 20% unit price can be slightly higher or lower than the contract — modest exposure for marginal upside.

Whichever package you choose, your energy will always be fully traceable. The matching engine settles every half-hour regardless of which tier you sit in.

§4 · Diversified pool

The second dial: assured terms.

On top of the fixed-price tier choice, generators choose whether to sit inside or outside the Diversified Pool. The Pool is UrbanChain stepping in as guarantor — if the consumer matched to your output fails to pay their bill, UC pays you anyway. There's a fee for that protection.

§6 · Next steps

From conversation to contract in three steps.

Once you've chosen your tier and Pool preference, getting from initial chat to live contract typically takes a couple of weeks. Three things need to happen — in this order.

01
Sign agreements

Standard NDA so we can see your portfolio data. Where applicable, an LOA (Letter of Authority) so we can fetch readings from the data aggregator on your behalf. Signed digitally — usually same day.

02
Portfolio information form

A short form covering your assets — capacity, location, technology, current offtake arrangements, any P442-eligible meter points. We use this to generate accurate revenue forecasts.
